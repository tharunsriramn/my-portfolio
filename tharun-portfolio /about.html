Conquer.

I started as a Mechatronics engineer in India. Then I moved to Canada, earned a Supply Chain Management diploma (Dean's List at Thompson Rivers University), and ended up managing a 54-vehicle Amazon delivery fleet from Burnaby.

That combination — engineering instinct plus supply chain strategy — shapes how I approach every problem. I don't just see a process; I see where it can break, where it can be automated, and what data it's not yet capturing.

At VCE Logistics, most of the systems I rely on today are systems I built myself. Not because there were no tools available, but because the right tool for a specific 54-van Amazon DSP fleet didn't exist off the shelf. So I built it. Usually in a week.

"If something needs a human to check it every day,
it needs to be automated."

I've applied that mindset to van rotation compliance, photo audits, end-of-shift reporting, mechanic prioritisation, dolley tracking, uniform inventory, and gas card distribution. Each time the result was the same: a system that runs itself and surfaces only what actually needs a decision.

There's one word I come back to constantly: Conquer. Not in a loud way — quietly. You show up, you understand the problem better than anyone else in the room, and then you make it irrelevant. That's what I try to do with every broken process I encounter.

Now I'm looking to move that thinking upstream — into supply chain strategy, network optimisation, procurement analytics, or operations excellence at a scale beyond 54 vans.
